Paraxial Ray Bending

An interactive walk-through of the Paraxial Ray Trace Equations — from Geary, Introduction to Lens Design, Chapter 4 (“Paraxial World”).

In the paraxial world every curved surface is replaced by a flat plane that still has power: it bends rays but adds no aberration. Two ray-trace equations do all the work — bending and transfer; the power equation just supplies each surface’s φ:

bending (Eq 4.1):  n′·u′ = n·u − y·φ
transfer (Eq 4.2):  y′ = y + u′·t
ingredient (Eq 4.4):  φ = (n′ − n)·C,  C = 1/R

y = ray height at the surface (measured from the optical axis) · u, u′ = ray slope before / after (u = tan U, U = angle to the axis) · n, n′ = index before / after · φ = surface power · C = curvature · t = gap to the next surface · y′ = height at the next surface.

Eq 4.1 says each surface subtracts exactly y·φ from the reduced slope n·u — so a ray through the vertex (y = 0) feels no power-bend, and taller rays bend more. When the ray stays in air (n = n′ = 1, e.g. a thin lens) the slope itself drops by y·φ.

Sign conventions

Heights y are measured from the optical axis, upward positive. Axial distances are measured from the surface, negative to the left — so an object a distance d in front of the lens sits at l = −d. A slope u is the tangent of the ray’s angle U to the axis, u = tan U (rising-to-the-right positive): a dimensionless rise per millimetre — never degrees — related to distances by u = −y/l, which is why tab 3 launches each ray with u = y/d. Paraxial optics is the small-angle (first-order) limit, where tan U ≈ U in radians — the sliders stop at |u| = 0.3 (≈ 17°) just to keep the pictures readable. Curvature C > 0 means the centre of curvature lies to the right of the surface (convex toward the incoming light); Eq 4.4 then decides the power — note the glass→air preset in tab 1: the same C gives negative φ when n > n′. With these signs a positive power φ > 0 in n′u′ = nu − yφ always bends rays toward the axis, whatever the sign of y.
